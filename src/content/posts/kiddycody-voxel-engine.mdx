My brother Mahdi, on that splendid fabulous amazingly cool of a day, decided that the best thing to do was starting a tutoring academy that teaches kids how to code. He's calling it **KiddyCody**. "So, Housam, _Brathar_. WE NEED A LOGO."

That was the whole ask. Fine, one morning of work, _in theory_. It turned into adventurous side quests and a C++ rewrite.

## The wrong tool (which turned out to be the right one)

A while ago, I had this specific tool in mind, the kind of tool that can make me free. Everyone nowadays frees themselves with gpt, talking nonstop and exploring the world. But why would I be free in 1D while I can be free in 2D? That time I created a canvas where you lay prompts out in space, and each one becomes a branch you can explore.

Here's roughly how it works:

- You start with a **seed prompt**, which is really a template. For logos the template was something like \_"Generate a logo for [___], colors [...], themes [...], style [...]."\_
- A `list` command chews on that template and spits out variants of the seed. Each variant is a branch on the canvas.
- So now I've got, let's say, 10 variants of the same prompt, each one filled in slightly differently.
- I hit the image-gen button on each. Out come 10 logo concepts.

It isn't really a logo tool. It's a general exploration thing whose whole point is generating "applets", and an applet can be basically anything, by my definition of course. So in any day was Whatever I needed. That day I wanted logos.

## We picked one, and then hit the wall

Out of the ten _(maybe 20? I don't recall)_, one concept clicked. We liked it enough to refine it directly in GPT and Gemini.

Then the problem showed up.

You can't actually control image generated assets the way a logo needs you to. I wanted to edit it granularly, change individual shapes. Resize cleanly, for favicons, hero banners, a t-shirt later. Repaint specific parts without regenerating everything and losing the bits I liked.

And I'm not a graphic designer. I don't know Illustrator. There are a few ways to do this properly with actual design tools, and I didn't have the patience for any of them _(This is a lie, I grinded the entire day learning an editing app that I ended up ditching after maybe 5 hours)_.

Hmmmmmmmmmmmm. Didn't work. That was it, am gonna program something that does.

## SVG blocks, with Claude Code

So I opened Claude Code and we rebuilt the concept as composable **SVG blocks**. A vector file, not an image.

The moment the logo existed as SVG, everything opened up. I could read the code. I could drop in new blocks wherever. Recolor one piece without touching the others. Resize with no fidelity loss.

I ran a few sample variants to sanity check it, and it was obvious right away that this was the right substrate.

## The engine, a CLI, and a loop with the LLM

I went all in on the engine. Started small. Nine colored blocks in a palette along the bottom, an isometric grid, click to place, right click to remove, Ctrl+Z to undo. Nothing fancy. Just enough to prove the grammar that worked in 2D still worked when I dragged it into 3D. I then out of not knowing what to do next, I built manually, block by block, a cozy house like we do in minecraft. I really enjoyed that, wait..... MY SECOND ahaaaaaaaaaaaaaa!!

I am lazy, so let me make an LLM do it for me.

Then the trick. Wire the CLI up to an LLM.

Give the LLM a library of building blocks and it can generate basically any structure you describe. You say what you want, the CLI composes blocks to build it. That's when it stopped being "a logo tool" and started feeling like a general structural composer. I decided after all this to adopt this as a long term project, and I will have future updates on its progress.

You can see in here how I generated a terrain with grass, cliffs, water, and trees, all from the block library and the CLI + LLM loop. I loved the isometric view of it, it really made the structure pop, and I intend to keep it that way.

## Performance. The web hit its ceiling.

From SVG blocks I went Into 3D. Into a 3D viewer. Into a first person mode. Slowly wanting to turn this into a game.

And then the web hit back. Bottlenecks everywhere. Render budget, memory, draw call dispatch, the usual story when you push a browser past what it was built for. I did every optimization I could reasonably do inside the browser.

It wasn't going to be enough.

## The call: rewrite in C++

So I made the call. Move to C++. Rebuild the engine properly. Do the research on Minecraft style optimizations (greedy meshing, chunk LOD, streaming, occlusion, the whole checklist) before writing another line of code. This trial is still ongoing and will need some more time to mature, but I aim to go the same route minecraft went, into this rabbit
hole.

## ENDING

I loved this. It felt like marching towards a real adventure into the voxel world. I have always wanted a touch into a Minecraft that I own, as I was always captivated by the infinitism it can provide, and the playability and emersion it can offer. Coding things with one's hands and shaping them into life is an art of its own, one that can't be understood unless practiced, and can't be comprehended unless touched. It really isn't about the keystrokes typed on the keyboard, but about the ideas and freedom one's own thoughts provide.

## A few things I keep coming back to

- The tool shapes the output. A canvas that lets you branch prompts in space gave me better exploration than any single chat I've ever had.
- Vectors beat pixels the moment you need to iterate on a brand asset. The SVG block pivot saved this whole thing.
- Good tools keep begging to be extended. If manipulating blocks with code is this easy for logos, it's this easy for anything structural.
- Know when to leave the browser. I like the web. But when you're chasing game grade performance, the right answer is a better runtime, not more optimization gymnastics.
